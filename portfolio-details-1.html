Project Details

  • Venetian plaster is a wall and ceiling finish consisting of plaster mixed with marble dust, applied with a spatula or trowel in thin, multiple layers, which are then burnished to create a smooth surface with the illusion of depth and texture.
  • Venetian plaster techniques include marmorino, scagliola, and sgraffito. When left un-burnished, Venetian plaster has a matte finish that is rough and stone-like to the touch. Un-burnished Venetian plaster is also very brittle and damages rather easily.
  • When applied correctly, Venetian plaster can be used to create a highly polished, rock-hard, marble-like finish. Venetian plaster is especially useful on surfaces where marble panels could not be installed easily, and on surfaces that would be too expensive to have carved from real marble such as columns, corbels, and curved walls.
  • Polished plastering is a technique that requires practice and patience to master.

Project Details

  • Polished plaster, also known as Venetian plaster, is a luxurious and elegant finish that adds depth and texture to interior walls. It's a technique that requires skill and attention to detail.
  • Once the plaster is dry, it's time to achieve the characteristic polished finish. Use a trowel or a specialized burnishing tool to smooth and compact the surface. This process compresses the layers and brings out the plaster's natural luster.
  • Gradually increase the pressure while burnishing to create a high-gloss or satin finish, depending on your preference.
  • After achieving the desired polish, apply a protective sealer designed for polished plaster. The sealer enhances the plaster's durability and resistance to moisture. Buff the surface gently with a soft cloth to enhance the shine and create a luxurious appearance.

  • Mix the scratch coat stucco material, which is typically a mixture of sand, Portland cement, and water. Some variations might include lime or other additives. Apply the scratch coat to the prepared surface using a hawk and trowel.
  • The scratch coat should be about 3/8 to 1/2 inch thick. Create a rough texture on the scratch coat using a scratcher tool or a stiff broom. This texture helps the next coat adhere.
  • Apply the brown coat over the scratch coat and shape it to the desired contours. This coat provides the structural strength and helps to achieve a smooth, level surface. Use a trowel and other tools to ensure the brown coat is even and properly aligned.
  • The finish coat is the final layer and provides the decorative appearance of the stucco surface.
  • Mix the finish coat stucco material, which is often a finer mix of sand, Portland cement, and sometimes additives for color or texture. Apply the finish coat to the brown coat using a trowel. Depending on the desired finish, you can achieve various textures, from smooth to rough.

Project Details

  • External restoration can involve various steps, and applying plaster is an essential part of the process.
  • Ensure that the surface is clean and free of loose paint, dust, debris, and any other contaminants. Power washing or sandblasting might be required.
  • Before applying new plaster, repair or replace damaged or missing sections of the building's exterior. This might involve patching holes, fixing cracks, and replacing broken or deteriorated plaster.
  • Wet the surface of the area where you'll be applying the plaster. This helps the plaster adhere better.
  • Apply a scratch coat (a base coat) of plaster to the prepared surface using a trowel. This coat provides a rough surface for the next coat to adhere to. Allow the scratch coat to partially set, but not fully dry, before proceeding.
  • Apply the finish coat of plaster over the scratch coat. This coat is usually thinner and is meant to provide a smooth and attractive surface. You can achieve different textures and finishes depending on your preference. Smooth, textured, or even decorative finishes are possible. Use a trowel to shape and smooth the finish coat, ensuring an even and uniform appearance.
